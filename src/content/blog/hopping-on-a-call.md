A message lands in Slack. "Hey, got a sec to hop on a call?" You look at the clock, at the thing you were in the middle of, at the thread of thought you'd just about pulled into focus. You type back, "sure." It is almost never a sec.

I don't hate any individual call. Calls, in the right place, are fine. What I hate is the reflex. The assumption that my time is ambient -- always available, always interruptible, always cheaper than writing three sentences. "Hop on a call" is a phrase that pretends to be casual while asking for something that isn't. It treats attention as a resource held in common, yours to draw from whenever the question in your head gets uncomfortable enough to share.

And the worst part is that it works. You do hop on. Because saying "could you write that out?" sounds standoffish, and because the social cost of pushing back is higher than the cost of losing another half-hour you can't get back.

---

The actual cost of that half-hour isn't the half-hour. It's the context switch on either side of it -- the ten minutes before where you start winding down so you can be present, and the twenty minutes after where you try to find the rope you were holding and discover it's on the floor somewhere. Cal Newport has written about this for years and I don't need to reproduce the argument. Anyone who does knowledge work for a living already knows, in their body, what a fragmented afternoon feels like.

Meetings also reward the wrong things. They reward the fastest talker, not the clearest thinker. They reward confidence over correctness. The person who walks in with a half-formed idea and a loud voice beats the person who would have written a better proposal if anyone had asked. "Alignment" very often means "I didn't want to write it down." "Sync" very often means "I wanted to feel like progress was happening." A lot of meeting culture is, honestly, theatre with calendars.

---

The counter-example that gets cited most, and deserves to, is [GitLab](https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/company/culture/all-remote/). GitLab is an all-remote company of over a thousand people, and their entire operating manual is published on the open internet. The handbook is the single source of truth. Decisions are written down. Proposals are merge requests. Discussion happens in issues. Meetings exist, but they're the exception, not the reflex. The cultural default is async, and the default is enforced not by yelling at people but by building the company around the written record.

The principle behind it is stark: if it isn't written down, it didn't happen. That sounds punitive until you work somewhere that operates the other way and watch the same decision get re-litigated three times in three different meetings because nobody bothered to put it in a document. Writing things down scales across timezones. It leaves an artefact the next person can read. It forces the person proposing the change to think it through before they take up anyone else's time. Those three things are, quietly, most of what "good communication" at a company actually means.

---

The other counter-example is older and, if anything, more extreme. The Linux kernel -- one of the largest and longest-running software projects in human history, running on roughly every server, phone, and toaster in the world -- is coordinated almost entirely through a [mailing list](https://lkml.org). Patches are sent as email. Code review is public, written, and archived forever. There are no daily standups on the kernel. There is no "let's hop on a call." There is a queue of patches and a lot of very opinionated prose.

Linus Torvalds has said, in various forms and over many years, that he prefers written communication because it forces people to structure their arguments, and because it lets him ignore the performance and read the substance. He's famously blunt on the list; he's also famously unavailable for phone calls. The kernel ships anyway. Arguably, it ships *because*. A project of that scale, with contributors scattered across every timezone and every employer on earth, cannot be coordinated in a meeting. Trying would collapse it. Writing is the only medium that holds.

---

The throughline in both cases isn't really about remote work, or tooling, or even time zones. It's about what writing does to an idea. Writing is how you find out whether the thing in your head is any good. A call lets a half-formed thought pass as a conversation -- you say it, someone nods, it sounds reasonable enough out loud, and the moment is gone before anyone has to examine it. A paragraph is less forgiving. A paragraph sits there. You read it back and you can tell, often immediately, that you don't actually know what you're proposing. That's not a failure of the paragraph. That's the paragraph doing its job.

Async-by-default is, quietly, a commitment to thinking before speaking. And to leaving a record the next person can read instead of re-deriving.

---

I'm not anti-meeting. There are things a meeting is genuinely the right shape for: hard conversations, early brainstorming when the problem is still mushy, the occasional moment where a team needs to hear each other's voices and remember they're working on the same thing. I've been in meetings that mattered. I know the difference.

What I'm against is the reflex. The default-to-call, the "quick sync," the afternoon eaten by half-hours. The question isn't "call or no call." The question is whether you respect your colleagues enough to write it down first, and whether they respect you enough to read it. Some things need voices. Most things need a paragraph.
